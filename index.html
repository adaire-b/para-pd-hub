I saw a gap, worked out what the system needed to look like, and built it. First year of teaching, no budget, no one asked me to.

The Problem

In March 2020, COVID-19 closed schools overnight. For classroom teachers, the pivot to remote instruction was disorienting, but at least there was a pivot to make. For paraeducators, instructional aides whose entire job is in-person student support, there was almost nothing: no clear way to contribute, no structured way to stay connected to the work.

I was in my first year of teaching at Enoch Elementary. The school had funds to keep paraeducators employed during the closure, but with that came a real operational requirement: their time had to be accounted for.

That created two needs at once:

Something to do

Meaningful professional development paraeducators could actually complete from home, on their own schedule.

Something to show

A way to document that the time they were being paid for was spent on real, relevant work.

What I Built

Over spring break 2020, unpaid and on my own time, I designed and built the Enoch Elementary Paraeducator Professional Development Hub: a self-service learning site on Google Sites that gave paraeducators meaningful development they could complete from home, on their own schedule. I had no prior training in instructional design, web development, or learning-management systems. The tool I had was Google Sites; the constraint was a feature, not an excuse.

The site covered six content domains, each mapped to the standards:

Reading

Acadience / DIBELS progress monitoring, UUSC Reading Foundations, S.P.I.R.E. structured literacy, Reading Rockets.

Math

Go Math and Touch Math organized by operation, Pre-K through upper grades, plus i-Ready training.

Behavior / SEL

Behavior-management strategies, Zones of Regulation, PBIS frameworks, and TAP video training.

English Language Learners

WIDA Can Do Descriptors, SIOP, Colorín Colorado, and video from the district ELL coordinator.

Exceptional Learners

Every IDEA disability category, from IRIS modules to CEC journal content, framed neurodiversity-first.

Paraeducator Skills & Info

Student privacy and FERPA, productivity tools, and medical-care procedures.

Key Design Decisions
Standards-first architecture

The homepage opened with the school’s “No Excuses” dispositions (Positive, Accountable, Willing to Help, Successful), mapped directly to the four Utah Paraeducator Standards. That wasn’t decoration; it was the organizing logic for the entire site. Every piece of content sat inside a competency framework.

Accountability, built into every resource

Every interactable element was paired with a Google Form reflection, placed under each resource and not just at the end. This was the operational core: paras were being paid during the closure, and their time had to be documented. It also happened to be better instructional design. They weren’t clicking links, they were producing a record of engagement.

Compliance-aware

Content drew from resources the district itself used, but this wasn’t an official district site, and I said so. Where ICSD portal training was still required separately, I noted it directly.

Audience-centered

Built for non-technical users with varied backgrounds. Organized by topic and grade level, the way paras actually work: jump to what’s relevant right now.

Stakeholder integration

The ELL section wove in video from the district’s ELL coordinator: identifying the right expert, making the ask, and fitting external content into the structure.

What Happened After

The site outlived the emergency. Once schools reopened, it stayed in use, first for onboarding new paraeducators, then as a standing reference during downtime. Paraeducators started calling it their “choose your own adventure” resource, a framing that was theirs, not mine, and told me the structure had fit.

The scope expanded, too. Originally built for special-education paraeducators, I redesigned it in coordination with the school’s reading and math specialists to extend it to reading and math parapros as well. That meant rethinking who the audience was, which content applied, and how the structure needed to shift.

The work also spread beyond my building. A companion S.P.I.R.E. reading-resource site I’d built for my own students proved useful to others, so I shared portions proactively through educator communities, reaching teachers outside the district and not just within it. I kept extending the hub, too, adding i-Ready training when the district adopted that platform.

What I’d Do Differently

The 2020 homepage surfaced the full competency text up front: every knowledge statement and skill descriptor at once. It’s accurate and thorough, but dense. Today I’d layer it: show the disposition-to-standard mapping first, and put the full competency text behind a click for anyone who wants the detail. The structure is right; the hierarchy needed one more design pass, and naming that is itself a sign of the design judgment I’ve built since.
